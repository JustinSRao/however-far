# Dialogue — how people talk in However Far

The game is written in English, but the world is Japanese (his path) and
Japanese-flavored anime-fantasy (her path, ADR-0014). We keep honorifics in dialogue the
way good anime localizations do: they carry real relationship information English can't.
**How a character addresses someone is a fact about their relationship** — treat changes
in address as story beats and record them in canon when they shift.

## Honorifics — what they mean and when to use them

Suffixes attach to a name: family name by default, given name when closer.

- **-san** — the polite default between people who aren't close: adults, strangers,
  acquaintances, classmates who aren't friends, shopkeepers, neighbors. Family name +
  san ("Nemoto-san") is neutral-polite; given name + san ("Itsuki-san") is warmer but
  still respectful. When unsure, -san is the safe choice — that's true for characters
  too, and a character who *over-uses* -san reads as distant or careful.
- **-kun** — mostly for boys/young men from someone equal or senior: classmates,
  teachers to male students ("Nemoto-kun"), a boss to a junior. Between teen peers it's
  friendly but not intimate. (Workplace seniors may also use -kun for junior women;
  rare in our high-school setting.)
- **-chan** — affectionate diminutive: small children, close female friends
  ("Suzu-chan"), family, sweethearts, pets, cute things. Using it for someone you're
  not close to is presumptuous — which can be a deliberate characterization.
- **-senpai** — an older student or senior in school/club/work. Works alone ("Senpai!")
  or attached ("Tōyama-senpai"). The junior counterpart **-kōhai** is a role word,
  almost never used as an address.
- **-sensei** — teachers, doctors, and accomplished professionals (writers, artists).
  Works alone or attached. Never self-applied.
- **-sama** — strong deference: customers, deities, royalty and high nobility. In her
  fantasy world this is everywhere at court ("Kaguya-sama", "hime-sama"). In modern
  Japan it's mostly service language or reverence; a teenager using -sama sincerely is
  comedy or worship.
- **-dono** — archaic, formal, samurai-flavored ("lord/master"); between warriors or in
  ceremonial address. Fantasy-appropriate (knights, old retainers); anachronistic and
  strange in the modern world.
- **No honorific (yobisute)** — family, lovers, and truly close friends. Unearned, it's
  rude or aggressive. **The move from "-san"/"-chan" to a bare name is an intimacy
  milestone, and being demoted back is an injury.** These transitions are prime story
  beats — never make them casually.

Family address: **onii-chan / onee-chan** (big brother/sister, affectionate),
**okaa-san / otou-san** (mother/father), etc. Younger siblings are usually addressed by
bare name.

## Family name or given name?

Default Japanese social distance: **family name + -san** until a relationship earns
more. Given-name address signals closeness (friends since childhood, family, romance).
So:

- Classmates who barely know Itsuki: **"Nemoto-kun"** (boys' peers/teachers) or
  **"Nemoto-san"**.
- His close friends: **"Itsuki"** or a nickname.
- Strangers/officials on his path: **"Nemoto-kun"** (adults to a teen boy) or
  **"Nemoto-san"**.
- A teacher: students call them **"[family name]-sensei"** or just **"Sensei"**.

## Our cast — fixed address conventions (canon)

- **Itsuki ↔ Suzune:** childhood friends and lovers — **bare given names**, both
  directions. As small children they were "Suzu-chan" and "Ii-kun"; those fossils
  resurface only in teasing or grief. Suzune deploys a mock-formal **"Nemoto-kun~"**
  when she's making fun of him. He has never once called her "Tōyama-san" — if he ever
  does, something is deeply wrong (usable beat).
- **On his path, after the vanishing:** nobody else remembers Suzune, so nobody else
  has a way to address her — hearing him say "Suzune" to people who don't know the name
  is part of the drama. Records/flashbacks use "Tōyama-san"/"Tōyama Suzune".
- **Maru the cat:** "Maru" or "Maru-chan" (pets take -chan; no family name, per the
  name-creator skill's exemption).
- **Her fantasy world:** courts run on **-sama** (royalty/the Villainess from her
  subordinates), **-dono** among knights and old houses, **-san** in villages. Suzune,
  as the summoned bearer of the strongest power, gets awed **-sama** from strangers —
  and it should make her uncomfortable every time: she is seventeen and wants to go
  home. Characters she befriends should graduate to "Suzune-san", then "Suzune" — chart
  those shifts deliberately.

## Craft rules (both paths)

- Honorifics are seasoning, not syrup: use them where address happens (greetings,
  summons, introductions, emotional pivots), not appended to every sentence.
- Don't translate them ("Mr. Nemoto" is wrong everywhere); don't stack them; never
  self-apply (-san on yourself reads as a joke about arrogance).
- Mismatched register is characterization: a noble who -sans a servant is unusually
  kind; a stranger who yobisutes Itsuki is a threat.
- Speech style carries voice even in English: sentence length, formality, what a
  character *won't* say. Suzune deflects weight with jokes then circles back; Itsuki
  under-speaks and lets actions answer.
- Subtext over statement: characters rarely announce their feelings plainly except at
  earned peaks (the river-road promise is the calibration point).

## Integration

- The World Writer prompt carries a compressed version of these rules; if you touch
  dialogue-related prompt text, keep honorific correctness and the address-shift-as-beat
  rule intact.
- Address shifts worth remembering ("the knight now calls Suzune by name") are canon
  facts — extract them.
- When unsure about an honorific edge case, prefer -san, or sidestep address entirely —
  wrong intimacy is worse than none.
